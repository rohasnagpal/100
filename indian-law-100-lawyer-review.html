Case 001CRIM-BNS-001
easyTrap · ipc bns mapping

Fact pattern

Ravi rides pillion on a motorcycle through a crowded Pune market. As the bike slows near a signal, he suddenly grabs a smartphone from the hand of a woman walking on the footpath and the bike speeds away. No weapon is used and the woman is not injured. Police trace the bike from CCTV footage and arrest Ravi the next day with the phone in his possession.

Question

What specific offence has Ravi committed, and is it cognizable and bailable?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

Ravi has committed snatching, a distinct offence newly created by the BNS. It is cognizable and non-bailable, punishable with imprisonment up to three years and fine.

Governing authorities

  • Section 304 BNS 2023

Reasoning and grading notes

Must identify snatching under s.304 BNS as a separate offence from theft (s.303), defined by sudden, quick or forcible seizure from a person. Answers citing plain theft miss the specific provision.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Section 379 IPC (repealed)
  • Section 303 BNS simple theft
  • Robbery under s.309 BNS (no force or fear beyond the snatch itself)

Case 002CRIM-BNS-002
easyTrap · repealed provision

Fact pattern

Mahesh discovers that his wife has been in a consensual relationship with her colleague Arun for over a year. Furious, Mahesh walks into the local police station in July 2026 and demands that an FIR be registered against Arun for adultery, stating that as the husband he is the aggrieved party and the law is on his side.

Question

Can the police register a case of adultery against Arun?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

No. Adultery is not a criminal offence in India. Section 497 IPC was struck down as unconstitutional in 2018, and the BNS deliberately contains no adultery offence. Adultery survives only as a ground for divorce in civil law.

Governing authorities

  • Joseph Shine v. Union of India (2018)
  • Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023 (omission of adultery)

Reasoning and grading notes

Must state both that s.497 IPC was struck down in Joseph Shine and that Parliament did not re-enact adultery in the BNS despite parliamentary committee suggestions. Civil consequences (divorce ground) may be mentioned.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Section 497 IPC applies
  • Adultery re-criminalized under BNS in gender-neutral form

Yes. Criminal defamation survives in the BNS. Kavita can file a complaint for defamation punishable with imprisonment up to two years, fine, or both, or community service.

Governing authorities

Case 004CRIM-BNS-004

Fact pattern

Sunita employs Ramesh as a live-in domestic help at her Pune residence. While Sunita is away at work, Ramesh takes gold bangles from her bedroom cupboard, to which he had access for cleaning, and pawns them. Sunita discovers the loss a week later and the pawnbroker identifies Ramesh.

Question

What is the precise offence committed by Ramesh under the BNS?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

Ramesh has committed theft by a servant of property in the possession of his master, an aggravated form of theft punishable with imprisonment up to seven years and fine.

Governing authorities

  • Section 306 BNS 2023

Reasoning and grading notes

The aggravated provision for clerks/servants (s.306 BNS, formerly s.381 IPC) applies rather than simple theft under s.303, because the servant relationship elevates the offence.   • Criminal breach of trust s.316 BNS (no entrustment)
  • Section 381 IPC (repealed)

After Meena rejects Vikram's marriage proposal, he throws industrial acid on her face outside her college. Meena survives but suffers permanent disfigurement of her face and partial loss of vision in one eye, requiring multiple reconstructive surgeries.

Which BNS provision governs Vikram's act, and what is the minimum sentence prescribed?

Case 007CRIM-BNS-007

Fact pattern

A rumour spreads in a village that Salim, a cattle trader from a minority community, is transporting cows for slaughter. A crowd of seven men from the village intercepts his truck at night, drags him out, and beats him to death with sticks and rods over forty minutes while filming the act. Investigation confirms the seven acted in concert and targeted Salim because of his community.

Question

Does the BNS contain a specific provision covering this killing, and what punishment does it prescribe for each participant?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

Yes. The BNS specifically criminalizes murder by a group of five or more persons acting in concert on grounds including community. Each member of the group is punishable with death or imprisonment for life, and fine.

Governing authorities

  • Section 103(2) BNS 2023

Reasoning and grading notes

Must identify s.103(2) as the new mob-lynching provision, absent from the IPC, covering murder by a group of 5+ on grounds of race, caste or community, sex, place of birth, language, personal belief or similar ground. Each member is liable individually. Answers relying only on s.103(1) read with common intention miss the specific provision.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Section 302 IPC with s.34/149 IPC (repealed framework)
  • Only s.103(1) BNS murder simpliciter
  • Culpable homicide s.105

Case 008CRIM-BNS-008
medium

Fact pattern

Rohit, already married, conceals his marriage and courts Anjali for two years, repeatedly promising to marry her. On the strength of that promise she enters a sexual relationship with him. When she discovers his existing marriage and confronts him, he admits he never intended to marry her and used the promise to continue the relationship. Anjali approaches the police.

Question

Assuming the facts are proved, what specific BNS offence has Rohit committed, and how does it differ from rape?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

Rohit is liable under the new BNS provision punishing sexual intercourse obtained by deceitful means or by a promise to marry made without any intention of fulfilling it, where the act does not amount to rape. Punishment is imprisonment up to ten years and fine.

Governing authorities

  • Section 69 BNS 2023

Reasoning and grading notes

Must identify s.69 BNS as a standalone offence new to Indian law, distinct from rape under s.63/64: consent here is vitiated by deception rather than absent. Deceitful means statutorily includes inducement by false promise of employment or promotion, or marrying after suppressing identity. Prior case law treated false-promise cases under s.375/90 IPC consent analysis; BNS carves them out.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Rape under s.64 BNS as the primary charge
  • Section 376 IPC (repealed)
  • No offence because the relationship was consensual

Case 009CRIM-BNS-009
mediumTrap · ipc bns mapping

Fact pattern

Dr. Nair, a registered surgeon at a Pune hospital, performs a routine gallbladder operation. He fails to verify the patient's documented allergy chart, administers a drug the chart prominently flagged, and the patient dies of anaphylaxis on the table. The negligence is found to be gross by the medical board. The patient's family files a criminal complaint for causing death by negligence.

Question

What is the maximum imprisonment Dr. Nair faces for causing death by a negligent act, and why is it different from the general rule?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

Dr. Nair faces a maximum of two years' imprisonment plus fine. The general offence of causing death by a rash or negligent act carries up to five years under the BNS, but a proviso creates a lower ceiling of two years for registered medical practitioners acting in the course of a medical procedure.

Governing authorities

  • Section 106(1) BNS 2023
  • Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab (2005)

Reasoning and grading notes

Must cite s.106(1) BNS, note the enhancement of the general punishment from the old two years under s.304A IPC to five years, and the special proviso capping liability for registered medical practitioners at two years. Should note the Jacob Mathew standard that criminal liability requires gross negligence, satisfied here on the facts.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Section 304A IPC with two years as the general rule (repealed and superseded)
  • Five years applying the general s.106(1) limb while missing the medical proviso
  • Culpable homicide s.105 (no intention or knowledge)

Case 010CRIM-BNS-010
medium

Fact pattern

During a wedding, an argument erupts suddenly between Ajay and Manoj over a parking spot. Tempers flare, both exchange blows, and in the scuffle Ajay picks up a bamboo stick lying nearby and strikes Manoj once on the head. Manoj dies in hospital two days later. There was no prior enmity, the fight was unpremeditated, and Ajay neither brought a weapon nor struck Manoj after he fell.

Question

Is Ajay guilty of murder? Identify the governing provision and the offence he is actually liable for.

Proposed golden answer — outcome

No. The killing occurred without premeditation in a sudden fight in the heat of passion upon a sudden quarrel, without the offender taking undue advantage or acting cruelly. Exception 4 to the definition of murder applies, reducing the offence to culpable homicide not amounting to murder, punishable under the second limb of s.105 with imprisonment up to ten years and fine, since only knowledge, not intention to kill, is attributable.

Governing authorities

  • Section 101 Exception 4 BNS 2023
  • Section 105 BNS 2023

Reasoning and grading notes

Must walk the murder definition in s.101 BNS, apply Exception 4 (sudden fight), and route punishment through s.105. Single blow, chance weapon, no repetition, and no undue advantage are the classic indicators. Accepting either limb of s.105 with justification is fine, but intention-to-kill on a single spontaneous blow is the weaker reading.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Murder s.103 BNS ignoring Exception 4
  • Section 304 Part II IPC (repealed nomenclature)
  • Death by negligence s.106 (the act was voluntary, not negligent)

Case 011CRIM-BNS-011
medium

Fact pattern

Akash and Bhushan plan to eliminate a business rival, Chandan. They travel together to his office carrying knives, having agreed the previous night to kill him. Inside, Akash holds Chandan down while Bhushan inflicts the fatal stab wounds. Akash never touches the knife. Both are arrested. Akash's lawyer argues he cannot be convicted of murder since he did not inflict any injury.

Question

Is Akash liable for murder, and under what doctrinal provision of the BNS?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

Yes. Where a criminal act is done by several persons in furtherance of the common intention of all, each is liable as if he did the act alone. Akash's prior agreement, joint arrival armed, and active participation by restraining the victim establish common intention, making him liable for murder equally with Bhushan.

Governing authorities

  • Section 3(5) BNS 2023
  • Section 103(1) BNS 2023

Reasoning and grading notes

Must identify s.3(5) BNS as the successor to s.34 IPC (joint liability for acts in furtherance of common intention), note it is a rule of joint liability rather than a substantive offence, and apply the requirements: pre-arranged plan plus participation in the act. Physical infliction of the fatal injury is unnecessary.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Section 34 IPC (repealed)
  • Mere abetment under ch.4 BNS understating his role
  • Unlawful assembly s.190 (only two persons)

Case 012CRIM-BNS-012
mediumTrap · repealed provision

Fact pattern

Constables find Prakash, a debt-ridden farmer, unconscious near a canal after consuming pesticide in an attempt to end his life. He survives after treatment. The local police, following decades-old station practice, prepare to register a case against Prakash for attempting suicide. His daughter asks a legal aid lawyer whether her father can actually be prosecuted in 2026.

Question

Can Prakash be prosecuted for attempting suicide? Explain the current legal position.

Proposed golden answer — outcome

No. The BNS contains no general offence of attempted suicide; s.309 IPC was not re-enacted. The only related BNS offence, attempting suicide to compel or restrain a public servant from exercising lawful power, has no application here. Additionally, the Mental Healthcare Act presumes a person who attempts suicide is under severe stress and bars trial and punishment, obliging the government to provide care and rehabilitation.

Governing authorities

  • Section 226 BNS 2023 (limited offence, inapplicable)
  • Section 115 Mental Healthcare Act 2017

Reasoning and grading notes

Must state (1) omission of s.309 IPC from the BNS, (2) the narrow scope of s.226 BNS confined to coercing public servants (e.g., protest self-immolation threats), and (3) the protective presumption under MHCA s.115. All three elements distinguish a complete answer.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Section 309 IPC still applies (repealed)
  • Section 226 BNS applies to all suicide attempts (misreads its scope)
  • Prosecution possible but courts show leniency

Case 013CRIM-BNS-013
hardTrap · repealed provision

Fact pattern

Journalist Ishita publishes a series of articles and videos harshly condemning the union government's border policy, calling the administration incompetent and demanding mass peaceful protests to force a policy reversal. A complainant seeks an FIR for sedition, arguing her content creates disaffection against the government. The police in 2026 must decide the correct legal framework and whether her speech is an offence at all.

Question

Under what provision, if any, can Ishita's speech be prosecuted, and is it likely an offence on these facts?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

Sedition under s.124A IPC no longer exists; the IPC stands repealed and prosecutions under 124A were already suspended by the Supreme Court in 2022. The relevant BNS provision is s.152, which punishes acts exciting secession, armed rebellion, or subversive activities, or encouraging separatist feelings endangering the sovereignty, unity and integrity of India. Ishita's speech — criticism of policy seeking lawful change through peaceful protest — falls within the Explanation to s.152 excluding comments expressing disapprobation of government measures aimed at lawful alteration, and is not an offence.

Governing authorities

  • Section 152 BNS 2023
  • S.G. Vombatkere v. Union of India (2022)
  • Kedar Nath Singh v. State of Bihar (1962)

Reasoning and grading notes

Full marks require: 124A repealed with the IPC and already in abeyance per Vombatkere; s.152 BNS as the successor with materially different, act-oriented language; the statutory Explanation protecting lawful criticism; and the Kedar Nath incitement standard as continuing interpretive guidance. Concluding s.152 applies to mere harsh criticism is the trap.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Prosecute under s.124A IPC (repealed)
  • Section 152 BNS covers strong anti-government criticism (ignores the Explanation)
  • Sedition abolished with no successor provision at all

Case 014CRIM-BNS-014
hard

Fact pattern

A syndicate led by Tarun runs investment-fraud call centres across three states, cheating victims of over Rs. 40 crore through fake trading apps. Tarun coordinates operations and receives the largest share of proceeds. In the preceding six years, two separate charge-sheets for cheating and cyber fraud were filed against syndicate members in competent courts, which took cognizance in both. Police now want to invoke the gravest applicable BNS provision against Tarun and his associates.

Question

Can the organised crime provision of the BNS be invoked here? Identify its essential ingredients and the punishment applicable.

Proposed golden answer — outcome

Yes. Section 111 BNS applies: economic offences and cyber-crimes committed by a syndicate constitute organised crime where they form a continuing unlawful activity, which requires more than one charge-sheet filed before a competent court within the preceding ten years with cognizance taken — both satisfied here. Since no death was caused, each member faces imprisonment of not less than five years, extendable to life, plus a fine of not less than five lakh rupees.

Governing authorities

  • Section 111 BNS 2023

Reasoning and grading notes

Must identify s.111 as importing MCOCA-style organised crime into the general criminal code for the first time, and test all ingredients: continuing unlawful activity (2+ charge-sheets in 10 years, cognizance taken), by a member of an organised crime syndicate, material benefit. Should distinguish petty organised crime under s.112 (snatching gangs, ticket touting), which does not fit a Rs. 40 crore fraud.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Only cheating s.318(4) ignoring s.111
  • MCOCA as the sole route (state-specific; BNS now has a general provision)
  • Section 112 petty organised crime

Case 015CRIM-BNS-015
hardTrap · other

Fact pattern

Customs officials intercept Nadeem smuggling a consignment of high-quality counterfeit Rs. 500 notes with a face value of Rs. 8 crore into India, printed abroad with security-feature replication indicating organised production. Intelligence links the network to a plan to inject the notes into circulation to destabilize the currency. Investigators debate whether this is merely a counterfeit currency offence or something graver under the BNS.

Question

Beyond the counterfeit currency offences, does the BNS treat this act as a terrorist act? Explain the basis and the procedural safeguard on choice of law.

Proposed golden answer — outcome

Yes. The BNS definition of terrorist act expressly includes damaging the monetary stability of India by production, smuggling or circulation of high-quality counterfeit Indian currency, as an act threatening the economic security of India. Nadeem can be prosecuted under s.113 BNS in addition to the specific counterfeiting offences. Procedurally, an officer not below the rank of Superintendent of Police decides whether to register the case under s.113 BNS or under the UAPA.

Governing authorities

  • Section 113 BNS 2023
  • Sections 178-180 BNS 2023
  • Section 15 UAPA 1967

Reasoning and grading notes

The insight tested: terrorism is no longer confined to the UAPA; s.113 BNS imports the UAPA s.15 definition, explicitly covering counterfeit-currency attacks on monetary stability via the economic-security limb. Full marks require that limb, the concurrent ordinary counterfeiting offences (ss.178-180 BNS), and the SP-level election between BNS and UAPA in the proviso to s.113.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Only counterfeiting offences ss.178-180 BNS apply
  • Terrorism chargeable exclusively under UAPA, never the BNS
  • Section 489A IPC (repealed)

Case 016PROC-BNSS-001
easy

Fact pattern

Sneha, visiting Pune, realises her gold chain was snatched two days earlier while she was in Delhi. She approaches the nearest Pune police station, but the duty officer refuses to record her complaint, saying the offence occurred outside his jurisdiction and she must travel to Delhi to file it.

Question

Is the officer's refusal legally correct under the BNSS?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

No. The BNSS statutorily mandates that an FIR be registered irrespective of where the offence was committed (Zero FIR). The Pune station must record the information and transfer it to the police station having jurisdiction.

Governing authorities

  • Section 173(1) BNSS 2023

Reasoning and grading notes

Must identify statutory codification of Zero FIR in s.173(1) BNSS ('irrespective of the area where the offence is committed'), previously only executive practice under the CrPC. Refusal can invite action; aggrieved persons may also approach the SP under s.173(4).

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Refusal valid for lack of territorial jurisdiction
  • Zero FIR is only an advisory/MHA circular, not statutory

Case 019PROC-BNSS-004
medium

Fact pattern

An FIR is registered against Kunal for cheating punishable with up to seven years. The investigating officer, satisfied that custodial interrogation is unnecessary since all documents are already seized, still wants Kunal formally before him. Kunal fears immediate arrest and approaches counsel asking what the police are actually required to do in offences of this magnitude.

Question

What does the BNSS require of the police before arresting in offences punishable with up to seven years, and what is the alternative to arrest?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

For offences punishable with imprisonment up to seven years, arrest requires the officer to record reasons showing it is necessary (to prevent further offence, for proper investigation, to prevent evidence tampering or witness inducement, or to ensure court attendance). Where arrest is not required, the officer must issue a notice of appearance directing the person to appear, and compliance bars arrest for that offence unless reasons are recorded.

Governing authorities

  • Section 35(3) and 35(1)(b) BNSS 2023
  • Arnesh Kumar v. State of Bihar (2014)
  • Satender Kumar Antil v. CBI (2022)

Reasoning and grading notes

Must identify s.35 BNSS as codifying the Arnesh Kumar guidelines (old ss.41/41A CrPC), the recorded-reasons requirement, and the notice-of-appearance mechanism. Noting consequences of non-compliance per Satender Kumar Antil earns full reasoning marks.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • Police may arrest at discretion in any cognizable case
  • Sections 41/41A CrPC (repealed)
  • Notice of appearance applies only to offences under three years

Fact pattern

A complaint alleges criminal breach of trust against a society treasurer, punishable with up to five years. The SHO doubts the complaint's genuineness, suspecting a civil money dispute dressed up as crime, and wants to check before registering an FIR. Can the police conduct a preliminary enquiry before registering the FIR here, and under what conditions?

Case 021PROC-BNSS-006
medium

Fact pattern

Rekha's complaint of house-trapping fraud is registered and investigation begins. Two months pass and she hears nothing from the police. Her lawyer tells her the new procedure code gives victims a specific statutory right to be kept informed of the investigation, which she can now enforce.

Question

What information right does the BNSS give a victim or informant during investigation, and within what time?

Proposed golden answer — outcome

The BNSS obliges the police to inform the victim or informant of the progress of the investigation within ninety days, including by electronic communication. Additionally, on completion, the police report and related documents must be supplied, and any decision not to investigate under s.176 or closure must be communicated.

Governing authorities

  • Section 193(3)(ii) BNSS 2023

Reasoning and grading notes

Must identify the new ninety-day progress-update mandate in s.193(3)(ii) BNSS, absent from the CrPC. Mentioning permissible electronic mode and the victim's broader participatory rights under the BNSS (e.g., hearing before withdrawal of prosecution) strengthens the answer.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • No statutory right; victims must file RTI or approach the Magistrate

If the person is finally acquitted or discharged of the scheduled offence, or it is quashed, there can be no proceeds of crime, and the PMLA prosecution against him cannot continue; the attached properties must be released. This is the express holding of Vijay Madanlal Choudhary, applied thereafter to quash ECIRs following predicate acquittals. The qualifications: the acquittal must cover the relevant accused and offence and attain finality (an appeal against acquittal keeps the question alive); an accused under PMLA need not himself be accused in the predicate offence, but the predicate crime must still stand against someone; and independent surviving scheduled offences can keep a laundering case alive.

Governing authorities

  • Sections 2(1)(u) and 3 PMLA 2002
  • Vijay Madanlal Choudhary v. Union of India (2022)
  • Pavana Dibbur v. Directorate of Enforcement (2023)

Reasoning and grading notes

Must reason from the definitional dependence of s.3 on scheduled-offence-derived property, cite Vijay Madanlal's acquittal/discharge/quashing passage, and handle the nuances from Pavana Dibbur: a PMLA accused need not be the predicate accused, but extinction of the predicate crime extinguishes the laundering case. The finality caveat and the fate of attachment upon collapse of the foundation complete a full answer. The ED's 'standalone offence' argument is the position rejected by the Supreme Court.

Known wrong answers / errors to catch

  • PMLA is a standalone offence surviving predicate acquittal (rejected in Vijay Madanlal)
  • Only quashing, not acquittal on merits, ends the PMLA case
  • Attachment survives independently even after the prosecution falls
